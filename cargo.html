Empty Cargo Containers

Why are Cargo Containers Empty?

Trade imbalance is the most important source in the accumulation of empty containers in the global economy. A region that imports more than it exports will face the systematic accumulation of empty containers, while a region that exports more than it imports will face a shortage of containers. If this situation endures, a repositioning of large amounts of containers will be required between the two trade partners, involving higher transportation costs and tying up existing distribution capacities. The region in this case could be within a continent or country, but can be also between two or more continents.

As different ports in the world become more crowded every year, storage space becomes more and more critical. Empty containers are stored while they wait for a modality to take them to a new point of cargo or port. Empty containers have to be stored for a short tem (at the terminal) or long term (in an empty depot) period, depending on the turnaround rate. The storage space required for empty containers will strongly depend on the dwell time of each container. The shorter the turnaround time of a container, the more preference a shipper has to store it at the terminal.

Our Proposed Solution:

Hydroponic Shipping Containers

About 90 percent of the world's goods are carried by sea, with more than 70 percent in shipping containers carrying everything from TVs to sportswear from Asia to the rest of the world. But the global imbalance in trade means most of these containers are empty on the return journey.

In order to stop this empty space going to waste, a collapsible hydroponic farm could grow vegetables inside the empty containers during the weeks-long sea voyage. The resulting plants could then be harvested at the following port, thereby providing vegetables to countries in need. This would put many of the world's millions of empty cargo containers to work while additionally feeding the world's population.

Green Alternative

Hydroponic shipping containers provide a green method of filling empty cargo containers. Empty cargo containers are a problem since they unbalance the weight of the ship. Filling them with plants would alleviate weight balancing issues.

Profit Potential

The food within hydroponic shipping containers can be sold at cargo ship destinations to generate profit and supply countries with food shortage with an easy to access food supply.

Reduced Stockpiles

Leftover cargo containers are a huge problem for high export countries. They often get discarded or are shipped back empty. Hydroponic shipping containers could fill these excess containers on return trips.

What Other Countries Are Doing

Country: United States

Idea: Using Wind for Small Deliveries

Description:

Taking advantage of the wind for small deliveries (e.g. through the use of balloons or parachutes) would provide a method for smaller countries who are unable to completely fill ships a way to fulfill order requirements. Additionally, smaller countries can potentially pool together cargo shipments into one shipment. This is similar to the airplane hub and spoke model.

Country: China

Idea: Foldable Containers

Description:

The use of foldable containers can lead to substantial net benefits in the total chain of container transport. In principle, these benefits increase as foldable containers are used on longer distances and through more links in the chain, because the costs of empty transport are reduced.

Country: Russia

Idea: Cross Use Cargo Containers

Description:

Similar to box shipping, a modularized approach to cargo containers could allow for cross use of containers for air transport. This would alleviate having to reship them on return trips. In addition, this can be combined with various sized containers to further modularize the process.

Country: Canada

Idea: High Powered Magnetic Latches

Description:

One of the largest problems with cargo containers is that they fall off during transport. This can be a danger to other ships as well as an environmental hazard to wildlife. High powered magnetic latches would help prevent cargo containers from falling off on the high seas and help boost delivary rates.
